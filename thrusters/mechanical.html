Mechanical System

The mechanical system of our project consists of a mechanical design segment, and a fluids segment. The mechanical design segment of the drone contains a compact 3D printed “brain”, which contains attachment points for all the electrical components, and 4 carbon fiber tubes, which act as the moment arms for thrust stabilization. The fluids segment of the drone consists of 2 two-liter bottle pressure vessels and 4 air-propelling nozzles, integrated with PTFE tubing.

Because aesthetic appeal was an important requirement for our project, the “brain” and nozzles were designed to seamlessly integrate the mechanical design and fluids segments. Gas flow out of the air tanks is passed through the carbon fiber tubes via internal tubing, and is ultimately expelled through the 2mm nozzle orifice, all of which are held to the carbon fiber tubes with friction screws. Importantly, the tanks are pressurized to 60 psi, but the drone system is run at 40 psi to ensure a more accurate control loop and air conservation. As we were not able to include an onboard regulator to regulate 40 psi in time, we ended up running the drone directly from the air compressor. Challenges

A key mechanical challenge we encountered was leakage throughout the fluid system. For example, initial iterations of the pressure vessels were not able to hold pressure for an extended amount of time, despite standard anti-leak protocols (sealing o-rings, teflon around the disconnects). A reliable solution to this problem was found through more accurate connections between the 3D printed bottle cap and disconnects. Specifically, ¼” NPT threads were modeled directly on the bottle cap, rather than tapped, which resulted in less internal damage to the bottle cap and a more robust, leak-free system.

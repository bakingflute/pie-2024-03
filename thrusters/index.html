Project Overview

Ghost Thrusters is the final project for class 'principles of integrated engineering (PIE)' aimed at developing a self-stabilizing drone using a reaction-control system.

The goal of this project is to create a drone capable of maintaining its equilibrium position during flight through the use of pressurized air thrusters. Our focus was primarily on the stabilization capabilities, so we designed a non-flight-capable drone that, when suspended in the air by strings, self-adjusts its position. This approach allowed us to concentrate on the balance and control systems without the added complexity of flight, providing a solid foundation for future development.

The development process involved a combination of electrical, mechanical, and software systems, each contributing to the drone's ability to stabilize itself. The electrical system was built around an ESP32 microcontroller, which interfaced with relays and solenoid valves to control the thrusters. An Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) was integrated to measure the drone's orientation and speed, providing real-time data critical for stabilization. On the mechanical side, we designed the structure of the drone to support the thrusters and accounted for the physics required to produce the precise amount of thrust needed for balance. This involved calculating forces and understanding the dynamics of airflow from the thrusters.

In the software domain, we developed code to parse IMU readings using I2C communication and implemented PID control loops. These loops processed the IMU data to adjust thrust and counter-thrust, ensuring the drone maintained its equilibrium. Before constructing the four-thruster jig, we simulated the entire system in Python to test and refine the code. The simulation helped us identify potential issues and fine-tune the PID parameters, accelerating the development process and ensuring smooth integration of all components.

By integrating the electrical, mechanical, and software systems, we created a cohesive prototype. The ESP32 controlled the relays and valves, while the IMU provided orientation data to adjust the thrusters. The mechanical design ensured proper thrust positioning, and Python simulations validated the code. This integration allowed the drone to stabilize itself when suspended in the air.

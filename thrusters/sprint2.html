Sprint 2

In the second sprint, we began designing the four-thruster drone in SolidWorks. This involved determining the optimal placement for the IMU and the prototype board, where all the circuitry would be mounted. Additionally, we redesigned the bottle caps for the air tanks, using plastic Fanta bottles, which proved effective in preventing gas leaks.

Once the design was complete, we sourced carbon fiber tubes, which we cut and used as arms to mount the thrusters. We then attached the valves, relays, and the IMU to the structure, ensuring secure placement for optimal functionality.

Electrically, we spent significant time debugging the issue of the Arduino resetting, using an oscilloscope for diagnosis. We discovered that the flyback voltage was too high, so we added a large inductor and capacitor across the ground and power lines of the relays to filter out voltage fluctuations. After addressing this issue, we proceeded to integrate the remaining two relays and valves for the four-thruster setup.

In terms of software, we developed a Python simulation to test the PID control loops for the four thrusters. This simulation not only focused on stabilizing the drone but also on enabling it to orient and maintain a specific angle. It allowed us to identify potential issues and fine-tune the PID parameters, significantly speeding up the development process and ensuring a smooth integration of all components.

As we move into sprint 3, our goal is to enhance the robustness of our circuitry by transitioning from a proto board to a more permanent setup, soldering all components in place. We will then focus on testing the software, fine-tuning the PID parameters to account for real-world factors, and applying these adjustments to the actual 4-thruster jig. Additionally, we plan to develop a test setup to suspend the drone, enabling us to better evaluate its performance and stability in a controlled environment.

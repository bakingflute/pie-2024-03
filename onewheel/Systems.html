Mechanical System

Disassembling Hoverboard

We started by disassembling a used hoverboard to repurpose two hub motors and a 39V lithium-ion battery. This gave us a solid foundation for the mechanical and electrical systems while saving costs and reusing existing parts.

Measuring Wheel Torque

To determine the wheel torque, we tested the wheel torque by clamping the scale to the edge of the wheel and running the motor at maximum PWM (40V). We measured the torque to be at least 7 N·m, but the clamp did not stay on the tire at higher torques. Based on the motor's 25mm stators with 4 wraps, we estimate a maximum torque of 12.7 N·m if drawing 15 amps.

Design

We explored the pendulum dynamics of the system to optimize platform height for stability. A lower platform acts like a stable pendulum but with less time for corrections, while a higher platform ​acts like an inverted pendulum with more correction time but unstable.

We also sketched the axle mount to ensure it could securely hold the wheel while integrating with the frame's beams, as well as the cross-sectional view showing where the deck, battery, and sensor should be positioned. These design considerations guided our testing and frame refinement process.

Wood Frame for Testing

To test platform height and optimize overall design, we built a wood frame and machined a metal block to hold the axle. 

We evaluated both upright and inverted pendulum systems by flipping the frame upside down and decided to proceed with the stable upright version. After further testing by standing on the platform, we decided to redesign the axle mount, bringing the platform closer to the axle for stability.

Metal Blocks and Axle

We machined a metal block to securely hold the axle and reinforced the frame by cutting and bolting metal parts. The axle mount is redesigned to bring the platform closer to the axle and is easier to ​attach and detach. Also, by reducing the size of the rectangle around the wheel, the frame became stronger.

Metal Frame and Assembly

For the final product, we built the metal frame using aluminum square tubes, including two 1.8 ft rails to support the wooden platform. ​For assembly, we made sure everything—hub motors, axle mounts, wooden platform, and metal reinforcements—fit together tightly with no gaps and stayed properly aligned for a strong, stable structure. The process involved drilling, screwing, bolting, and sanding.

Mounting Battery and Electrics

We carved out a space in the wooden platform to fit the battery, ensuring it sits securely and level with the frame. We then mounted the battery, Arduino, and motor controller to the bottom of the platform. This keeps the electronics protected, even when someone stands on the platform, and maintains a lower center of mass for better balance. We also ensured clean wiring to prevent any interference with moving parts.

Electrical System

For detailed schematics and components, refer to this page.

Overall Connections

Our electrical system's key connections include the motor driver, hub motors, gyro sensor, motor shield, Arduino microcontroller, and the battery.

The 39V battery powers the system and connects to the motor driver, which controls the hub motors. The Adafruit Motor Shield V3 sits on top of the Arduino microcontroller, providing an interface to send PWM signals for motor speed and direction control. The gyro sensor (MPU9250) is soldered onto the motor shield and communicates with the Arduino via I2C to provide real-time tilt angle data. The Arduino processes this data and adjusts the PWM output to the motor driver, ensuring smooth operation and balance corrections. All components work together seamlessly to deliver power, read sensor inputs, and control the hub motors dynamically.

Motor Driver Selection

To control the hub motors, we chose the RioRand 350W 6-60V PWM DC Brushless Electric Motor Speed Controller. This motor driver supports a wide voltage range and high power, making it compatible with the OneWheel's 39V battery and motor requirements. It provides precise control over motor speed and direction using PWM signals from the Arduino.

39V Lithium-Ion Battery

The 39V Lithium-Ion battery powers the entire OneWheel system. ​We repurposed this battery from an existing hoverboard, so it’s already designed to handle high current and continuous motor operation.​ It provides the right voltage and capacity to run the hub motors while keeping a good balance between weight and runtime.

We connected the battery to the motor driver for steady power delivery. For safety, we used crimp connectors to prevent short circuits and keep everything reliable.

IMU and Gyro Sensor

The MPU9250 is an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) that combines a 3-axis accelerometer, 3-axis gyroscope, and 3-axis magnetometer. This sensor detects the tilt of the OneWheel. The Arduino microcontroller reads data from the MPU9250 via the I2C communication protocol, calculating the board's pitch and balance angles.

Hub Motors and Hall Sensors

The hub motors efficiently provide the propulsion needed to drive our OneWheel, high torque at low speeds which is crucial for maintaining balance and acceleration. These are brushless DC motors compactly integrated into the wheel hub. They are paired with Hall sensors to monitor motor position and speed.

We rely on the motor driver to interpret Hall sensor feedback and adjust the PWM signal accordingly, ensuring the motors operate smoothly during balance corrections.

Soldering and Wiring

We focused on proper soldering and wiring to simplify connections and ensure compact design for the durability and reliability of our OneWheel's electrical system.

Battery Connections: Soldered ​crimp connectors for secure and easy connections to the motor driver.
Motor Wires: Direct soldering to ensure minimal signal loss and ​reliable electrical contact.
Sensor  Wiring: Soldered directly onto the motor shield (Adafruit V3 Motor shield).
Heat Shrink Tubing : Applied over soldered joints to insulate and protect connections.

PWM Motor Speed Control Testing

We first tested motor control using a simple PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) oscillation example that controls motor speed and direction. It gradually increases the motor's PWM duty cycle from 0% (stopped) to 100% (full power) and then decreases it back to 0%. When the duty cycle reaches zero, the motor's direction is reversed using the PIN_DIR signal. The PWM signal, controlled via PIN_PWM, adjusts the motor's speed by varying the power sent to it, while the direction pin changes the wheel's rotation direction. This logic simulates a smooth ramp-up and ramp-down of motor speed. The DELAY variable sets how quickly the speed ramps, and the oscillation ensures the motor runs forward and backward continuously for performance testing.

Reading Motor Speed 

Then we programmed to read the current motor speed, that uses the pulseIn() function on the SPEED_PIN (Pin 12) to measure the time a signal remains HIGH, which represents the motor's rotational period. From this, the motor frequency is calculated, and the RPM (revolutions per minute) is derived using the motor's gear ratio (1:1) and wheel circumference (28.27 in.). The speed is then converted into practical metrics like MPH (miles per hour) and KPH (kilometers per hour). This allows to monitor the real-time speed of the wheel, ensuring accurate feedback for performance analysis and optimization.

Reading Gyro Sensor Values

This code is for reading tilt angles using the MPU9250 gyro and accelerometer sensor. The getTiltAngle() function communicates with the sensor via the I2C protocol to retrieve accelerometer values along the X, Y, and Z axes. These raw values are mapped to a range between -90° and 90°, representing the tilt angles. By applying trigonometric calculations, the function derives angles for all axes, allowing to monitor the board's orientation. The tilt angle (X-angle) is particularly critical for detecting balance shifts in the OneWheel, providing real-time feedback for the self-balancing mechanism.

PID Control Function

The PID control function ensures precise adjustments to motor speed based on tilt angle deviations. The pidControl() function calculates the error as the difference between the desired setpoint (0°) and the current angle. It applies proportional (kp), integral (ki), and derivative (kd) components to compute the motor PWM signal, balancing responsiveness and stability. Proportional control reacts to the current error, integral control mitigates cumulative errors, and derivative control accounts for sudden changes. This PID-controlled output determines motor speed and direction, adjusting power dynamically to maintain the OneWheel's balance. By combining PID logic with tilt readings, the system can correct instability and maintain self-balanced.

Integrated Code

This code integrates motor speed measurement, tilt angle detection, and PID control to implement the real-time balancing system. It reads tilt angle from the gyro sensor and adjust motor speed and direction based on the setpoint and current tilt using PID. In the main loop, it continuously computes the tilt angle, adjusts motor speed and direction based on the PID output, and stops the motor when the brake switch is pressed. The motor direction and speed are updated in real-time.
